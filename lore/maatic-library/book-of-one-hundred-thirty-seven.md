# The Book of One Hundred and Thirty-Seven

*A slender treatise bound in copper-stained linen, kept on the shelf of the Order's natural philosophers. The number 137 is embossed on the spine in plain figures.*

---

Of all the numbers the world uses to hold itself together, one is the most jealously guarded, and the most thoroughly inexplicable. It is approximately **one over one hundred and thirty-seven** — `α ≈ 1/137.036…` — and the Order calls it **the keystone constant.**

This number measures how strongly light couples to matter. It is the reason atoms hold together at all. Make it slightly larger, and atoms collapse into their own nuclei; the world unmakes itself in a flash of coherence. Make it slightly smaller, and atoms fly apart into their components; nothing complex enough to think a thought ever assembles. The cosmos as the Order knows it — the cosmos in which there *is* an Order, in which there *are* heroes asking questions — exists because this single constant landed exactly where it landed.

Every philosopher who has handled this number has confessed it as the deepest mystery they encountered. One left a famous note: *"a magic number that comes to us with no understanding."* Another died with the number written on the wall of his sickroom, beside the words *"why this?"* The Order does not pretend to answer the question. It teaches, instead, that the number is *the place where the cosmos's choices are most exposed* — the spot where, if there is a Maker, the Maker's hand is closest to the surface.

A hero who comprehends this book earns Illumination, and a quieter awe. Some Order priests carry a small token marked **137** — a pewter charm, a tattooed numeral, a knot of three strands and seven beads — to remind themselves that they live, and are able to wonder, only because the number landed where it did.
